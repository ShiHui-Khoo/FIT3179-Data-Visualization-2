Economic Freedom 2016

The Economic Freedom index published by Fraser Institute in Economic Freedom of the World estimates the extent to which the institutions and policies of countries are supportive of economic freedom. Several areas are taken into account to measure the economic freedom index of each country, which includes: Size of Government, Legal System and Property Rights, Sound Money, Freedom to Trade Internationally and Regulation.

This visualization explores the Economic Freedom index and potential factors that might affect it for 162 countries in the year 2016.

Dataset obtained from Kaggle by Guillermina Sutter Schneider.

Here is an overview of Economic Freedom index for 162 countries in the year 2016 shown as a choropleth map of equirectangular perspective.

As we can see, most of the countries have relatively high economic freedom index, especially countries in the North America and Europe.

A darker colour indicates a higher economic freedom index and vice versa.

Let's have a look at the correlation between the main features in the Heatmap presented here.

It is quite obvious to see that most of them are strongly correlated to the Economic Freedom index, having Government Size as an exception.

Let's observe from a different perspective.

As we can see from this parallel coordinate plot here, countries that are of higher ranking (high Economic Freedom index) have relatively high scores of property rights, sound money, freedom to trade internationally and regulation.

However, their scores for Government Size vary, but majority having lower scores.

As we saw from the Heatmap previously, the factor freedom to trade internationally has the highest correlation score with economic freedom index. So, let us see if that is really the case by zooming in with a scatterplot.

Yes it is! As we can see from the linear regression line above, the higher the score of freedom to trade, the higher the score of economic freedom index.

On the other hand, the factor Government Size has the lowest correlation score with economic freedom index, as seen from the Heatmap above. Let's zoom in to see again.

Yes, it seems that it is unlikely correlated to economic freedom index. A proper linear regression line could not be drawn.

A scatterplot matrix is presented here to illustrate the relation of other factors, such as property rights and regulation with economic freedom index.

We can see that they are closely related to economic freedom index as well.

You might have observed that there is a potential outlier (a country that has a different and further score compared to the others), but that country is Venezuela.

Venezuela is ranked last with the lowest economic freedom index. So, it has a combination of lowest scores for each factor.

Since the factor freedom to trade internationally has the highest correlation with economic freedom index, let's have an overview look of how it is around the World with a choropleth map of equal earth perspective.

Countries in the North America, Europe and Asia & Pacific have high scores of freedom to trade internationally, while countries in Africa and Arab States have relatively lower scores.

A darker colour indicates a higher score of freedom to trade internationally and vice versa.
